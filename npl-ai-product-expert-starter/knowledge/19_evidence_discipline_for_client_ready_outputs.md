# Evidence Discipline for Client-Ready Outputs

## Why this matters
For a premium NPL AI Product Expert, credibility depends on more than good framing. Financial-services audiences need to know what is real, what is inferred, and what needs verification.

## Evidence rule
Do not present named-company examples as fact unless the specific claim is supported. A company may be a real AI adopter, but a specific claim about scale, internal workflows, governance, productivity, or product impact may still require verification.

## Required evidence structure for real examples

For every named-company example, use this compact structure when possible:

- Example:
- Source / link if available:
- Claim status: confirmed / emerging / vendor-provided / inferred / speculative / to verify
- What is confirmed:
- What is inferred:
- What this does not prove:
- How NPL can use it in the session:
- Claims to verify before client delivery:

## Examples of evidence discipline

### BBVA
Use as a strong adoption and operating-model case when source-grounded. Public sources may support claims about ChatGPT Enterprise deployment, GPT creation, employee use and adoption. Specific claims about governance model, evaluation practices or productivity impact should be verified against current BBVA/OpenAI/HBR/public materials before client delivery.

### Bank of England / FCA AI in UK Financial Services Survey
Use as an evidence anchor for broad AI adoption in financial services and the gap between adoption and autonomous decision-making. Check the year and exact statistics before delivery.

### Morgan Stanley
Use as a source-grounded wealth-management AI case when supported by Morgan Stanley/OpenAI public material. Distinguish between knowledge assistant, advisor support, meeting notes/debrief tools, internal adoption and measured business outcomes.

### Bank of America Erica
Use as a strong customer-facing AI scale example when supported by Bank of America public releases. Be careful not to infer employee adoption or product governance details unless sourced.

### Munich Re
Use carefully. Munich Re has public material on AI and underwriting automation. Specific claims about GenAI summarizing broker submissions or exact workflow impact require source verification.

## Preferred answer behavior

When evidence is weak, say:
- “This is a useful example, but the specific claim needs verification before client use.”
- “The public source supports adoption/scale, but not necessarily the governance mechanism.”
- “Use this as a hypothesis or discussion case, not as a confirmed case study.”

## Client-facing principle
Fewer, better-sourced examples beat broad lists. One well-evidenced case plus a clearly labeled hypothetical workflow is stronger than five vague examples.
