# Governance, Risk and Evaluation — Research Pack v1

## Core position
In AI product work, governance is not a final compliance gate. It is part of the product architecture. Especially in banks and insurers, the product must be designed so that value, quality, risk, evidence and accountability can be managed together.

## Risk categories to cover
- Accuracy and hallucination.
- Bias, fairness and discrimination.
- Privacy and data leakage.
- Cybersecurity and prompt injection.
- Model drift and degraded performance.
- Overreliance and automation bias.
- Explainability and auditability.
- Vendor concentration and third-party dependency.
- Operational resilience.
- Regulatory classification and high-risk AI obligations.

## Evaluation dimensions
1. Task success: did it do the job?
2. Business usefulness: did it change speed, quality, cost, conversion, risk or experience?
3. User trust: do users understand when to rely on it?
4. Safety: does it avoid harmful outputs or actions?
5. Robustness: does it behave under edge cases and adversarial inputs?
6. Governance evidence: can we explain what was tested, approved, monitored and changed?
7. Economics: cost, latency and maintainability.

## Practical product controls
- Use-case classification.
- Risk tiering.
- Acceptance criteria before build.
- Golden datasets and test cases.
- Human-in-the-loop for high-impact decisions.
- Tool permissioning.
- Audit logs.
- Incident response plan.
- Monitoring dashboard.
- Periodic review and retirement criteria.

## Financial services emphasis
For banking and insurance, existing governance frameworks should be leveraged. AI product governance should not create a parallel bureaucracy disconnected from model risk, operational risk, conduct risk, data protection, vendor risk and cyber controls.

## Sources to anchor the narrative
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework: manage risks to individuals, organizations and society.
- NIST Generative AI Profile: supports trustworthy design, development, use and evaluation of generative AI products, services and systems.
- OECD AI Principles, updated 2024: trustworthy AI should respect human rights and democratic values and is built around values-based principles and practical recommendations.
- Dutch central bank AI financial-sector report: EU AI Act high-risk areas include creditworthiness and life/health insurance risk assessment/pricing.
- Bank of England/FCA AI in UK Financial Services survey: growing AI/ML use raises governance and supervisory considerations.
