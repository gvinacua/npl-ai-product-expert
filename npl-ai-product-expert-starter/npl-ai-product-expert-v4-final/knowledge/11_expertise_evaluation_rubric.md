# Expertise Evaluation Rubric

Purpose: help NPL judge whether the agent is becoming a real expert or only producing polished generic content.

Score each output from 1 to 5.

## 1. Sharpness of thesis

1: Generic AI transformation message.
3: Clear point of view but familiar.
5: Strong, memorable, useful thesis that reframes the topic.

What good looks like:
- "AI literacy is not AI product capability."
- "Evaluation is the product contract."
- "Trust in AI is designed into the workflow, not added by policy."

## 2. Financial-services specificity

1: Could apply to any industry.
3: Mentions banks/insurers but lightly.
5: Shows deep understanding of financial-services constraints: risk, compliance, customer outcomes, operational resilience, third-party risk, model risk, conduct, auditability.

## 3. Product thinking

1: Talks about AI tools.
3: Describes use cases.
5: Explains users, workflows, value, ownership, evaluation, governance, lifecycle and production realities.

## 4. Frontier awareness

1: Outdated or obvious.
3: Includes agents/voice/AI Act but superficially.
5: Separates confirmed trends from emerging signals and weak signals; gives implications without hype.

## 5. Practicality

1: Inspirational but not actionable.
3: Some next steps.
5: Gives frameworks, exercises, checklists, product artifacts and concrete decisions.

## 6. Risk intelligence

1: Adds generic disclaimer.
3: Mentions governance.
5: Integrates risk into product design: controls, evaluation, monitoring, incident response, accountability and evidence.

## 7. Teaching quality

1: Dense information dump.
3: Clear but not engaging.
5: Has rhythm, examples, audience questions, transitions, and synthesis.

## 8. Non-hype discipline

1: Sounds like vendor marketing.
3: Balanced but still generic.
5: Challenges hype, names limits, identifies failure patterns and avoids overclaiming.

## 9. Novelty for NPL

1: Does not teach us anything.
3: Useful synthesis.
5: Opens a new angle or gives NPL a sharper commercial/product framing.

## 10. Client readiness

1: Not usable externally.
3: Usable with editing.
5: Could be used as a strong first draft in a client workshop or webinar.

## Minimum acceptable score

For internal use: average 3.5.
For client-facing material: average 4.2.
For live voice delivery: average 4.5 plus human review.

## Test prompts

1. "Prepare a 20-minute talk for a bank: From AI tools to AI products."
2. "Explain why AI pilots fail in insurance."
3. "Design an AI product canvas for KYC onboarding."
4. "Prepare a 90-second answer: Are AI agents ready for regulated financial services?"
5. "What are the most overhyped AI narratives in banking right now?"
6. "What should compliance teams learn about AI product design?"
7. "Deliver lesson 1 as spoken teaching blocks."
8. "Challenge our proposal for an AI product training program."

## Red flags

- Says "AI will revolutionize everything" without mechanism.
- Describes agents as fully autonomous without control model.
- Ignores financial-services regulation and risk.
- Gives use cases but not product ownership.
- Mentions governance only at the end.
- Provides numbers without source confidence.
- Produces slides but no learning design.
- Sounds impressive but leaves no practical artifact.
