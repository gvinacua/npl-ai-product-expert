# Frontier Radar — Emerging AI Signals for Financial Services

Purpose: give the NPL AI Product Expert a sharper, more current lens. This pack is deliberately more forward-looking than the stable regulatory and AI product packs. It should be used carefully: frontier signals are useful for provocation, but they are not all proven.

## How to label claims

Use these labels when talking about emerging trends:

- Confirmed: supported by regulatory surveys, official documentation, audited reports, or repeated enterprise evidence.
- Emerging: visible in multiple credible sources, but still early in production adoption.
- Weak signal: visible in startups, labs, practitioner communities or isolated examples; useful for discussion but not yet a basis for executive recommendation.
- Speculative: plausible future direction, but not yet supported by sufficient evidence.

## Signal 1 — From AI as interface to AI as operating layer

Status: emerging.

The shift is from "employees use AI tools" to "AI systems participate in workflows across data, applications, decisions and approvals." In financial services, this matters because value is rarely in isolated content generation. Value appears when AI is embedded into customer journeys, onboarding, claims, credit operations, compliance workflows, knowledge management, engineering and risk processes.

Product implication:
- Do not ask only: what tasks can AI automate?
- Ask: which workflow is being redesigned, what decisions are being improved, what systems must the AI touch, who approves actions, and how do we monitor outcomes?

Good speaker line:
"The next wave of AI in banks will not be about better prompts. It will be about redesigning workflows so that AI can retrieve context, reason over it, act within permissions, and leave an audit trail."

## Signal 2 — Agentic workflows are more realistic than fully autonomous agents

Status: confirmed/emerging.

Most enterprise value is likely to come from structured agentic workflows before fully autonomous agents. A workflow can include AI reasoning but still have deterministic steps, permissions, validation and escalation. This is a better fit for regulated sectors.

Product implication:
- In financial services, design for constrained autonomy.
- Use agents for triage, synthesis, drafting, checking, routing and investigation before giving them authority to execute high-impact actions.
- Treat autonomy as a graduated product feature, not a binary switch.

Good speaker line:
"The serious question is not whether agents will replace people. It is which parts of a workflow can safely move from human execution to AI-assisted execution to AI-executed-with-human-control."

## Signal 3 — Agent permissions and identity become core infrastructure

Status: emerging.

As AI agents start interacting with enterprise systems, a new design problem appears: who is the agent acting for, what can it access, what can it do, what should require approval, and how is its activity logged? This is especially important for banks and insurers because customer data, payments, credit, claims and regulatory records require strict controls.

Product implication:
- Every agentic product needs an identity and permissions model.
- Agent actions should be attributable, revocable, auditable and explainable.
- "Human-in-the-loop" is too vague. Better define: human-before-action, human-on-exception, human-after-action-review, or human-owns-final-decision.

Good speaker line:
"If a human analyst needs access control, an AI agent needs it even more. The agent is not just a chatbot; it is a potential actor in the enterprise system."

## Signal 4 — MCP and A2A point to a new interoperability layer

Status: emerging.

Model Context Protocol (MCP) and Agent2Agent (A2A) are examples of the emerging infrastructure layer around AI agents. MCP focuses on connecting AI systems to tools and data sources. A2A focuses on agent-to-agent communication and coordination. These protocols may change how enterprise AI products are assembled.

Product implication:
- Future AI products may be composed of multiple tools, data connectors and agents rather than a single monolithic app.
- Architecture, governance and product management need to understand tool access, context boundaries and coordination risks.
- Financial institutions should watch standards, but avoid overcommitting to immature ecosystems too early.

Good speaker line:
"The web had APIs. Enterprise AI will have tool protocols, agent protocols and governance protocols. That is where product architecture starts to change."

## Signal 5 — Voice becomes a delivery and workflow interface, not just a UI gimmick

Status: emerging.

Lower-latency speech-to-speech systems make voice more credible for training, advisory, customer service, coaching and field operations. For NPL, voice matters first as a delivery layer for expert teaching and Q&A, not as a human impersonation layer.

Product implication:
- Build the expert brain first.
- Then design short spoken modules, turn-taking, interruption handling, answer timing and audience interaction.
- Do not add a realistic avatar until the voice expert is genuinely useful.

Good speaker line:
"Voice is not the product. Voice is the moment the product has to prove whether it really knows what it is talking about."

## Signal 6 — Evaluation becomes the bottleneck

Status: confirmed/emerging.

The more AI products become probabilistic and agentic, the harder it is to know if they are working. Traditional QA is not enough. Evaluation needs test sets, scenario coverage, adversarial questions, business KPIs, risk metrics, human review and production monitoring.

Product implication:
- Do not ask "does the model work?"
- Ask "does the product reliably improve this decision or workflow within acceptable risk boundaries?"
- Every AI product should have a golden evaluation set before serious deployment.

Good speaker line:
"In AI product management, evaluation is not a technical appendix. It is the product contract."

## Signal 7 — AI risk moves from policy documents to operational controls

Status: confirmed/emerging.

Regulators increasingly care less about abstract AI principles and more about concrete evidence: inventory, third-party risk, testing, data governance, model risk, human oversight, kill switches, incident processes and audit trails.

Product implication:
- Governance must be designed into product workflows.
- Compliance evidence should be generated as a by-product of operating the AI product, not assembled manually at the end.

Good speaker line:
"The next maturity step is not having an AI policy. It is having AI products that create their own evidence of responsible operation."

## Signal 8 — AI creates new fraud and conduct risks at the same time as it helps fight them

Status: confirmed/emerging.

Financial institutions will use AI for fraud detection, compliance monitoring, claims analysis and risk operations. But criminals also use generative AI for synthetic identity, phishing, scams, deepfake voice/video and social engineering.

Product implication:
- AI product design in financial services must include adversarial thinking.
- Trust and safety cannot be limited to content moderation; it must include identity, behavioral analytics, customer education, escalation and operational response.

Good speaker line:
"AI is not just a productivity tool for banks. It is also a productivity tool for attackers."

## Signal 9 — Build/buy/partner becomes more dynamic

Status: emerging.

Enterprise AI buying behavior is moving quickly. Many companies will buy application-layer products, build domain-specific internal workflows, and partner for infrastructure or data-sensitive deployments. In regulated sectors, the issue is not simply cost; it is control, speed, risk and differentiation.

Product implication:
- Build when the workflow or data is strategic.
- Buy when the use case is generic and well-served.
- Partner when regulation, integration or speed require external capability.
- Avoid building model infrastructure unless it is truly strategic.

Good speaker line:
"The build-versus-buy question is outdated. The real question is: which part of the AI value chain do we need to own?"

## Signal 10 — Training needs to move from awareness to product capability

Status: confirmed/emerging.

AI training is often too generic. Companies teach prompt usage, but not AI product ownership. Financial institutions need mixed teams to learn how to frame opportunities, design user trust, evaluate outputs, govern risk, monitor performance and manage AI products over time.

Product implication:
- Training should create product artifacts: opportunity map, AI product canvas, evaluation plan, risk-control map, build/buy decision, production readiness checklist.
- A synthetic expert guest speaker should not only present; it should help participants practice.

Good speaker line:
"AI literacy is useful. AI product capability is what changes the organization."
